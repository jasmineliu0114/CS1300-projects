How Accessible are Radio Buttons?

Radio buttons may seem like simple UI components, but their design can significantly affect how people engage with digital forms and settings. In this project, I examined how different radio button implementations shape user experience across platforms and input types. I was particularly interested in how visual and interaction design impact usability for people using mice, keyboards, touchscreens, and assistive technologies.

Inputs

To better understand how people interact with radio buttons, I looked closely at three common interfaces, Google Forms, Amazon Checkout, and Outlook Settings, across multiple input modes. This process helped me examine the subtle differences in what users can see, feel, and do depending on their device and context. I paid special attention to whether actions were intuitive, efficient, and consistent. Each interface told a different story about what it means to design with accessibility and learnability in mind.

These signals are crucial for building interfaces that respect all users, especially those with different abilities or preferred modes of interaction.

State Models

To make sense of the input-output dynamics, I modeled the radio button states for both mouse and keyboard users in Google Forms. Then, I redesigned these models, making tradeoffs in accessibility, learnability, memorability, or efficiency. These model diagrams helped visualize states and transitions, revealing how small changes in design can make an interface feel more approachable or more frustrating.

Reflection

After watching Kat Holmes' Inclusive Design, we reflect on the observations and revision made above. Here are the key points regarding usability and accessibility of the different design choices.

  • The radio button components provided clear visual feedback using hover effects, focus indicators, and active states, which help to improve discoverability.
  • Keyboard navigation was also clear, as tab and arrow key interactions allowed keyboard users to interact effectively.
  • The redesigned radio buttons are designed with even stronger visual indicator, not only highlighting elements more clearly for users who may have low vision, but also making it clearer that both radio buttons and their associated labels can be clicked to select/deselect.
  • Standardizing the user flow between different input states by giving mouse, keyboard, touchscreen, and voice reader users the choice to select/deselect benefits both efficiency and accessibility.
  • One positive accessibility impact of radio button selection method, particularly for Amazon Checkout and Outlook settings is a clear focus box for keyboard users to ensure they know which radio button they are selecting.
  • A negative accessibility impact of auto-selection using arrow keys is that users who accidentally press an arrow key may change their selection without realizing it.
  • Mouse and keyboard users seem to get the most prioritization, which touchscreen and users can face some inconsistencies. While screen-readers seem to be giving user all the necessary information, the organization of that information seem to be more of an afterthought. This could affect the user experience for those who may rely on an input method and make certain applications harder for them to use.

Conclusion

This project deepened my understanding of how even the most familiar components like radio buttons can offer complex challenges and design opportunities. Through comparing multiple platforms and input types, I learned to look beyond functionality and ask: how do users actually experience this? Small visual cues, input flexibility, and consistent feedback all profoundly shape that experience.

What surprised me most was how much the context of use and method of interaction could change what felt intuitive. Designing for mouse users is not the same as designing for keyboard, touchscreen, or screen reader users, and accessibility is often unevenly considered across these modalities. Creating state models and redesigning components pushed me to think systemically and empathize with users who interact in ways I might not normally consider.
